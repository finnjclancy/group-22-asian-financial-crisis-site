Introduction

High growth masked a fragile financial architecture

The economies at the center of the crisis had posted years of rapid growth and were widely treated as development success stories. The report’s central argument is that those headline growth numbers hid a more dangerous setup: banks borrowing in dollars, lending long in local currency, and operating in systems with weak prudential oversight.

Once confidence broke, financing vanished, currencies fell, and balance sheets that had looked manageable under fixed or tightly managed rates became unworkable.

Why this crisis still matters

  • It forced a rethink of capital flows and exchange rate regimes.
  • It exposed how moral hazard and weak governance amplify shocks.
  • It reshaped how the IMF designs crisis programs and social safeguards.
  • Its lessons still inform debates on emerging-market resilience.

Conclusion

The main lesson is not that growth failed, but that it hid leverage and fragility

Rapid growth can conceal bad balance sheets

Growth above 7 percent did not offset the risks created by short-term foreign borrowing and weak banking supervision.

Exchange-rate regimes shape crisis dynamics

Pegged systems can reassure markets for a time, then intensify the break once reserves and credibility run out.

Crisis management is always a trade-off

IMF-backed adjustment restored confidence, but not without deep short-run pain. Malaysia’s alternative reduced some of that pain while creating different costs and controversies.

The institutional reforms mattered

More flexible exchange rates, stronger supervision, and lower external vulnerabilities made the region more resilient later on.
